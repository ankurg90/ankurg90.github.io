How Claude Code Helped Us Solve a Fluids and Electrokinetics Problem

Ankur Gupta and Arkava Ganguly

An account of using AI to solve a mathematical chemical physics problem, including where it stumbled.

TL;DR: Claude Code helped us solve a problem in fluid mechanics and electrokinetics that we had been working on. We published a preprint on this result that is being submitted for peer review. We are sharing this because it was a highly technical problem that Claude was very capable at solving, though it made mistakes. Details below.

What Is Electrophoresis?

If you aren't familiar with electrophoresis, the basic idea is to understand how fast a charged particle would move in response to an electric field when it is suspended in an electrolyte solution (think table salt in water). Imagine a tiny charged particle, say a protein, a nanoparticle, or a colloid, suspended in salty water. You switch on an electric field. The particle starts to move. How fast it moves relative to the field strength defines a quantity called the mobility. One of my colleagues calls mobility the "bang for your buck": it tells us how much we can drive the motion of the particles.

This is an important topic because electrophoresis is helpful for a range of problems. It is used in gel electrophoresis to separate DNA and proteins, in measuring the surface charge of nanoparticles (the "zeta potential"), in microfluidic devices for sorting cells, and in characterizing colloids for drug delivery and environmental remediation.

A charged particle in an electrolyte. When an electric field is applied, the particle drifts while the surrounding ion cloud exerts a drag. Only nearby ions are shown for simplicity.

The mathematical description is elegant. The velocity $U$ of the particle is:

$$U = M \times E_\infty, \qquad M = \frac{\varepsilon_f \zeta}{\mu}\, f_H(\kappa a)$$
The mobility $M$ depends on: the fluid permittivity $\varepsilon_f$, the particle's surface potential $\zeta$ (zeta potential), the fluid viscosity $\mu$, and a function $f_H$ that captures how thick the ion cloud is ($\kappa^{-1}$, the Debye length) relative to the particle size ($a$).

The function $f_H(\kappa a)$, known as Henry's function, smoothly connects two classical limits. When the ion cloud is very thick compared to the particle ($\kappa a \to 0$, the "Hückel limit"), $f_H = 2/3$. When the ion cloud is very thin ($\kappa a \to \infty$, the "Smoluchowski limit"), $f_H = 1$.

The Smoluchowski Surprise: Shape Doesn't Matter?

One of the key results in electrophoresis is that the mobility is shape-independent. It basically argues that you can have any shape of the particle, and it would always have the same mobility value. A sphere, a cube, a rod, a crumpled piece of paper: they all move at the same speed, as long as they carry the same surface charge.

In the Smoluchowski limit, particles of any shape move at the same speed, but only when the ion cloud is much thinner than the particle.

This is a well-known result and there was even debate about it when it was first proposed. Hückel (1924) calculated a mobility that was only 2/3 of Smoluchowski's value. It turned out both were correct, just in different limits. Hückel's result applies when the ion cloud is very thick; Smoluchowski's when it is very thin. Henry (1931) later bridged them with the interpolation function $f_H(\kappa a)$.

However, the shape-independence only works in the so-called thin double-layer limit. The double layer is the length scale at which oppositely charged ions get electrostatically attracted. For many real systems (nanoparticles, proteins, particles in low-salt solutions), the ion cloud can be comparable in size to the particle itself. In that regime, shape should matter.

Our Question: What If Shape Does Matter?

How would shape matter in electrophoresis?

We realized that to answer this, we need to relax the thin double-layer assumption. However, relaxing it presents challenges since most of the ways to calculate mobility only work with the thin double-layer assumption. We, therefore, leaned on a framework from our prior work (Ganguly & Gupta, 2024) that allowed us to circumvent this assumption by keeping the full volume of the ion cloud in the calculation.

We focused on slightly deformed spheres because that is analytically tractable. Think of a slight deformation of a sphere. In this limit, you can create a range of shapes but still preserve them under the same mathematical construct. The particle surface is described by:

$$r_s(\theta) = a\bigl[1 + \varepsilon\, f(\theta)\bigr], \quad f(\theta) = \sum_n c_n P_n(\cos\theta)$$
The shape is a sphere of radius $a$ plus a small deformation $\varepsilon f(\theta)$, where $P_n$ are Legendre polynomials, which are mathematical building blocks for describing shape variations. The coefficient $c_2$ controls elongation/flattening, $c_3$ controls pear-like asymmetry, and so on.
A sphere smoothly deforming through different shapes: prolate (football), oblate (M&M), and pear-shaped. Each is built from a different combination of Legendre modes.

What We Found

We quantified the impact of shape on mobility. Our main result is a compact formula for how shape modifies the mobility:

$$C_\parallel = f_H(\kappa a)\bigl[1 + \varepsilon\, c_2\, \sigma_2(\kappa a)\bigr]$$
The mobility of a deformed sphere is Henry's function for a sphere, multiplied by a correction that depends on the shape ($\varepsilon\, c_2$) and a new "shape correction factor" $\sigma_2(\kappa a)$ that we computed.

Three key findings emerged:

1. Shape correction is smooth and predictable.
The correction factor $\sigma_2$ starts at $+1/5$ when the ion cloud is thick (where the only effect of shape is to change the drag on the particle) and smoothly goes to zero when the cloud is thin (recovering Smoluchowski's shape-independence). A prolate (football-shaped) particle can move up to ~6% faster than a sphere in thick ion clouds.
2. Only one type of deformation matters.
Of all the ways you can deform a sphere, only the "elongation vs. flattening" mode (the $P_2$ or quadrupolar mode) affects the mobility. Pear shapes, bumpy shapes, wavy shapes: they are all electrophoretically silent. The electric field simply cannot "see" them. This is a consequence of angular selection rules: the dipolar electric field can only couple to the quadrupolar shape mode.
3. Our results match known exact solutions.
For the special case of spheroids (ellipsoids of revolution), exact solutions were computed by Yoon & Kim in 1989. Our perturbation theory agrees with their results quantitatively, including a subtle non-monotonic behavior for oblate particles.

Checking Our Work

We validated our results against the exact spheroid solutions of Yoon & Kim (1989). A spheroid is a special case of our deformed-sphere framework: an ellipsoid of revolution maps onto a pure $P_2$ deformation. Electrophoretic Silencing

To drive home the point that only the $P_2$ mode matters, consider four particles that look very different but are grouped into two pairs sharing the same elongation ($c_2$). Within each pair, the particles have different higher-order features ($c_3$), yet produce identical mobility curves.

Four particles grouped by their $c_2$ value. Despite looking very different, particles with the same elongation have identical mobility curves. Higher-order shape details are "electrophoretically silent."

How We Used Claude

This is a relatively complex problem that requires a lot of careful analytical work.

First attempt: Claude Sonnet 4.6 (extended thinking)

We initially used the Claude chat option and used the Sonnet 4.6 extended model to try to get the solution. It arrived at a solution, but that was incorrect. The key portion that it struggled with was that we gave it a prompt of trying to solve for the first correction to the thin double layer limit, but it kept using the Stone and Samuel formula, which is only valid for the limit where Smoluchowski exists. This made it go in a loop, and after much digging, we figured that the solver went in that loop. It could have been that our prompt was not sufficiently clear.

Second attempt: Claude Opus 4.6

By getting inspired by the blog of Prof. Schwartz, we decided to upgrade to Opus 4.6. Here, we first gave it a much clearer prompt, and we asked it to repeat the analysis in our prior work for an arbitrary Debye length, but for deformed spheres. The results were immediately improved. Then we switched to Claude Code on VSCode and investigated everything step by step. We also provided guidance on how to do consistency checks. It initially understood the assignment but made mistakes. The key mistake was that it assumed an incorrect coefficient from the Brenner 1964 paper. This was fixed after some prodding. We also used Claude Code to create all the figures, refine the figures, write the paper, and refine everything.

Our Thoughts on AI in Research

  • It is very powerful.
  • It needs to be used with caution as it can confuse even the experts.
  • Guardrails are needed on its use. Undergraduate and graduate training needs to be thoughtful so as to not offload thinking.
